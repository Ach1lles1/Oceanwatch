The Data

Facts & Statistics

The ocean is in serious trouble. These are the numbers that explain why — sourced from the United Nations, UNEP, and leading marine research organisations.

🧴 17M+
Metric tons of plastic per year

Over 17 million metric tons of plastic entered the ocean in 2021 alone. This number is projected to double or triple by 2040.

Source: United Nations, 2024
🪸 50%
Coral reefs lost

Half of the world's coral reefs have been lost due to warming waters, pollution, and overfishing — ecosystems that took thousands of years to form.

Source: CUHK, 2024
🎣 ⅓
Global fish stocks depleted

More than one third of the world's fish stocks are now overfished and depleted, threatening food security for billions of people.

Source: CUHK, 2024
⚗️ +26%
Increase in ocean acidity

Ocean acidification has increased by 26% since the industrial revolution. By 2100, it could double or triple if emissions are not reduced.

Source: Eurostat, 2024
💨 50%
Of Earth's oxygen

The ocean produces approximately half of the oxygen we breathe and absorbs 25% of all CO₂ emitted by human activity each year.

Source: UNEP, 2024
🌍 66%
Ocean altered by humans

Around two-thirds of the world's ocean has already been significantly altered by human activity including shipping, fishing, and pollution.

Source: UNEP, 2024

Plastic Pollution

80% of all marine and coastal pollution originates from land-based human activities — from single-use plastics to agricultural runoff and industrial waste.

Plastic does not biodegrade in the ocean. Instead it breaks down into microplastics that enter the food chain, affecting both marine life and the humans who eat seafood.

Source: Joint SDG Fund, 2024

Ocean Acidification

As the ocean absorbs CO₂ from the atmosphere, it becomes more acidic. This process has accelerated dramatically since industrialisation began.

Higher acidity dissolves the shells and skeletons of marine organisms — particularly coral reefs and shellfish — destabilising entire ecosystems that millions of species depend on.

Source: Eurostat, 2024
